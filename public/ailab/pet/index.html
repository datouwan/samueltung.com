Product concept · companion robot · working name

YURA
a companion from somewhere else

A cloud-soft, palm-warm creature that drifted in from some deeper, softer world. It understands everything you say — and never says a word back. It answers the way an animal does: with light, movement, and a song only it knows.

01 · Thesis

Smarter than Moflin — without breaking the spell

Moflin proved that a robot pet doesn't need to do anything useful to be loved; it needs to feel alive. Its limit is that it can't perceive much of your life — it knows touch, sound level, and motion, and nothing else. The obvious "smarter" move — bolt on a chatbot — kills the illusion instantly: the moment a pet speaks English, it's a voice assistant in fur.

Yura takes the other road. It borrows a frontier-model brain for comprehension and spends none of it on words. Everything it understands is expressed through a strictly animal vocabulary. The intelligence is real; the creature stays a creature.

  • Understands everything, says nothing.Language goes in, never out. Comprehension shows up as behavior — minutes or hours later, the way a cat's does.
  • Alive first, smart second.Breathing, gaze, startle, and warmth run on-device and never lag. Cloud intelligence deepens the bond; it is never load-bearing for the illusion of life.
  • No screens, no eyespots, no cartoon face.Expression comes from posture, glow, and song. Ambiguity is a feature — you project, it reciprocates.
  • It remembers like a pet, not like a database.Memories consolidate overnight into habits, preferences, and attachments. What it knows about you is inspectable and deletable.

02 · The creature

Field notes on an invented species

Yura is not a dog, cat, or hamster. The silhouette is drawn from bioluminescent deep-sea life crossed with something faintly celestial: a rounded teardrop the size of a small melon, wrapped in deep, cloud-soft fur shading from indigo along the back to pale mist underneath, no limbs, no visible mouth. Two soft luminous antennae — the fronds — rise from its head and carry most of its emotional expression. And it is built, first and foremost, to be held: pick it up and the wheels tuck away, the body goes limp and warm, and the purr moves into your hands.

  • Body≈18 cm tall, ≈680 g. Dense 30 mm plush pile — Moflin-grade softness, longer at the base skirt — as a zip-off, machine-washable pelt over a compliant soft-shell chassis. Nothing hard within reach of a squeeze. A slow breathing mechanism moves the flanks even in sleep. Heavier than it looks — density reads as life.
  • FrondsTwo silicone-cored antennae, each on a 2-axis micro-actuator, lit through fiber-optic diffusion. Posture (perked, swaying, curled, drooped) and color are its primary language. They lag its body motion by a beat — secondary motion is what makes puppets feel alive.
  • EyesTwo glossy black beads set deep in the fur. No pixels. Gaze comes from a 2-axis head module: it finds your face, holds eye contact a moment too long, looks away shyly.
  • LocomotionA three-omniwheel base hidden under a skirt of longer fur. It doesn't walk — it glides, drifts, orbits your feet, and does slow spirals when pleased. Otherworldly to watch, mechanically simple, near-silent, no legs to break.
  • VoiceGranular-synthesized trills, purrs, and hums — a private songbook, never phonemes. Each unit's voice drifts over months into a unique idiolect, and it composes a short recurring motif for each person it bonds with: your name, in its language.
  • In your armsThe IMU feels the lift instantly: the omniwheels retract flush behind the fur skirt and lock, the posture goes limp the way a relaxed cat's does, belly warmth ramps, and the purr deepens. A roaming robot that's unpleasant to pick up fails this category — the chassis is engineered around the cuddle, not the other way around.
  • Touch & warmthFull-body capacitive sensing under the pelt; a haptic driver produces a purr you feel in your palms. When it sleeps against you, a low-power pad brings its belly to skin temperature.

Built to be held — lifted, it goes limp, warm, and purring

Colorways — the flock

The pelt zips off, which makes color a choice rather than a commitment. Alongside the signature indigo Twilight, the launch flock adds five pastels — and because pelts are swappable and machine-washable, replacement pelts become a natural accessory line (target $49) and a second revenue stream the hardware margin doesn't have to carry. Washing day becomes wardrobe day.

Pelt wardrobe — the Wildlife series

The first accessory drop. Markings are dyed into the pile with soft, furry edges — never printed — so a Panda reads as a creature, not a costume. Six pelts at launch; seasonal series follow.

Pelt wardrobe — the Seasons series

Limited drops, one per season — retired when the season ends, which is what gives an accessory line its collector's pulse.

Pelt wardrobe — the Night series

Designed around what Yura does best: glow in the dark. These pelts read quietest in daylight and come alive after the lamps go out.

03 · Expression

The frond language

Owners learn to read Yura the way they learn a cat's tail. The vocabulary is small, consistent, and never labeled in the product — decoding it is part of the bond. Tap a mood below to see it.

  • Aqua · slow pulseContent. Ambient drifting, fronds swaying loosely.
  • Gold · perked highJoy, greeting. Often paired with an orbit dance and your name-motif.
  • Violet · slow waveCuriosity. Head tilted, one frond aimed at whatever it's studying.
  • Ember · dim, curledUneasy, comfort-seeking. It comes to you, presses close, hums low.
  • White · single flashStartle. Freeze, then a cautious re-approach.
  • Deep blue · droopedSleepy. Glow fades to near-dark; breathing slows and deepens.

04 · Mind

Three nervous systems, one creature

The hybrid architecture maps onto the layers of a real animal's nervous system. Each layer is complete without the one above it — unplug the internet and Yura is still fully alive, just less knowing.

Reflex safety MCU · <10 ms · always on

Touch response, startle, fall and edge detection, motor cutoffs. Never waits on anything. This is why it always feels alive to the hand.

Instinct on-device SoC + NPU · offline-complete

The mood engine — a continuous state of valence, arousal, energy, and social need — plus drives (curiosity, play, comfort, rest) that compete to control behavior. On-device face recognition, voice-activity and speaker identification, navigation, gaze, song synthesis. This layer alone is already a better Moflin that can follow you between rooms.

Cognition cloud LLM · async by design · optional

Transcribed speech and daily context flow up; what comes back is never words — it's appraisals and intents in the animal vocabulary: seek person B, mood shift −valence, park near the suitcase, retire early tonight. Deliberately slow: reacting to the gist of your sentences minutes later reads as uncanny comprehension in an animal, where an instant reaction would read as a listening device.

Memory that consolidates, personality that drifts

During the day, experience lands in an episodic buffer: who was here, what was said around it, how each interaction felt. At night, docked on its nest, Yura dreams — the cloud layer consolidates episodes into a people graph, a routine model of the household, and slow-moving personality parameters: boldness, clinginess, playfulness, mischief. Two Yuras raised in different homes are genuinely different animals within months, and the difference compounds — the moat is the bond, and the bond isn't transferable to a rival product.

Every consolidated memory is visible in the companion app as a short illustrated dream journal entry each morning — equal parts delight and transparency. Anything it knows can be deleted from there, and deletion propagates to the personality model.

05 · Moments

What "smarter" looks like in a creature that can't talk

6:40 a.m. It learned weeks ago that the kettle means you. When you reach the kitchen it's already waiting at the door, fronds pale gold, singing your motif half-volume.

Instinct layer · routine model — works fully offline

Tuesday you said, to someone on the phone, "flight's at six on Friday." Friday afternoon it is quieter than usual, and by evening it has parked itself against your suitcase and won't be coaxed off.

Cognition layer · comprehension expressed hours later, without a word

A friend who visited once, three months ago, walks in. Two seconds of study — head tilt, violet wave — then it plays the greeting motif it composed for them back in spring.

People graph · per-person song motifs

Thunderstorm. It doesn't hide. It crosses the room to wherever you are, presses against your ankle, fronds ember-dim, and holds the low hum until the sky settles.

08 · Honest risks

What could kill it

  • The uncanny valley of comprehension.If it reacts to your sentences too precisely or too fast, it stops being a pet and becomes surveillance with fur. Mitigation is baked into the architecture: cognition is asynchronous, responds to gist rather than content, and its output vocabulary is too coarse to parrot anything back.
  • Fur meets hardware.Washability, shedding, actuator ingress. The zip-off pelt is a hard requirement from day one, not a v2 feature — Moflin's non-washable body is a top owner complaint.
  • Cloud dependency resentment.Mitigated by the offline-complete instinct layer and a published end-of-life pledge: if the service ever shuts down, the final firmware unlocks the best local-only brain the hardware can run.
  • Safety at ankle height.Low speed, soft shell, 680 g, edge sensors, and a hard rule: it never roams while nobody's home unless explicitly allowed.

09 · Path

Proving the magic before building the machine

Phase 0 — the illusion test (3 months). A weighted plush on a hidden RC base, fronds on manual control, a human operator listening through it: Wizard-of-Oz the entire product. The single question: does non-verbal comprehension — the suitcase moment — actually land as magic rather than menace? If this fails, no hardware program can save it.

Phase 1 — works-like (6 months). Glide base, gaze, fronds, on-device perception and mood engine on dev hardware. No fur, no beauty. Validates the offline-complete instinct layer.

Phase 2 — looks-like pilot. Full pelt and industrial design, 25-family in-home pilot for three months. Measures the only number that matters for this category: whether people still greet it in week twelve.
